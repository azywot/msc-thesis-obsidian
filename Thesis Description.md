### Title: Small Agents, Big Impact

#### Description:  
This Master’s thesis investigates whether ensembles of smaller, cost-effective agents can match or exceed the performance of large pretrained models in multi-agent systems when combined through intelligently designed interactions. Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong capabilities in agentic planning, reasoning, and autonomous decision-making, but they are often resource-intensive. In contrast, groups of smaller language models (SLMs), effectively coordinated, may provide comparable performance with significantly lower computational cost.

The thesis will explore:

1. Performance trade-offs between large monolithic models and systems composed of multiple cooperating smaller agents.
2. Design and coordination strategies that allow SLM agents to collaborate effectively on complex tasks.
3. Efficiency and scaling laws, including cost, latency, and memory use, to better understand how multi-agent systems behave under resource constraints.
4. Failure modes and robustness of multi-agent systems compared to large single models, identifying strengths and limitations in real-world scenarios.  

In addition, this research will examine how augmenting agents with external tools - such as web search, code execution environments, and structured memory modules - influences reasoning and planning performance, building on recent work demonstrating substantial benefits from such integrations.

Where appropriate, the thesis will investigate fine-tuning approaches to adapt smaller models and tool-augmented agents to domain-specific tasks, and analyze how targeted adaptation impacts both individual agent competence and system-level collaboration.

This research aims to contribute to a deeper understanding of how practical, resource-efficient agentic AI systems can be constructed, balancing performance with real-world constraints.
